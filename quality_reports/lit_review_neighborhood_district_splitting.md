# Literature Review: neighborhood district splitting

- **Date:** 2026-04-07
- **Query:** neighborhoods, communities of interest, and district splitting

## Summary

This paper sits between two literatures that are usually adjacent rather than
integrated. The first asks how researchers should measure neighborhoods and
local context. That literature emphasizes that Census geographies are often poor
proxies for meaningful local communities, especially across very different urban
settings. The second concerns redistricting criteria and communities of
interest, where keeping socially meaningful places together is often invoked as
an objective but is difficult to define or operationalize.

The contribution here is descriptive and concrete. Using the new city-defined
neighborhood dataset of Ansolabehere et al. (2025), the paper measures how
often one observable and locally meaningful boundary is preserved by two common
district systems: state legislative districts and city council districts. The
paper does not claim that neighborhoods exhaust the meaning of community of
interest, but it provides an interpretable benchmark for a concept that is often
discussed abstractly.

## Key papers

### Ansolabehere et al. (2025) — City-defined neighborhood boundaries
- **Main contribution:** Introduces a large new dataset of city-defined
  neighborhoods in the United States.
- **Method:** Collects and harmonizes neighborhood boundaries for 206 large
  cities and links them to Census-block demographic data.
- **Key finding:** City-defined neighborhoods differ substantially from common
  Census proxies and are useful for place-based research.
- **Relevance:** This is the core data source and measurement foundation for the
  paper.

### Wong et al. (2020) — Maps in People's Heads
- **Main contribution:** Validates a measure of subjectively meaningful local
  context drawn directly by respondents.
- **Method:** Survey-based map drawing with validity and reliability checks.
- **Key finding:** People carry coherent and politically meaningful mental maps
  of community that are not reducible to standard administrative units.
- **Relevance:** Supports the idea that locally meaningful boundaries deserve
  separate measurement.

### Cross (1998) — Communities of interest and minority districting
- **Main contribution:** Distinguishes competing conceptions of communities of
  interest in redistricting.
- **Method:** Conceptual and legal analysis centered on *Miller v. Johnson*.
- **Key finding:** Geographic communities of interest and broader group-based
  communities need not align.
- **Relevance:** Helps frame neighborhoods as one observable kind of community
  boundary rather than the only one.

### Barabas and Jerit (2004) — Redistricting principles and racial representation
- **Main contribution:** Examines how traditional districting criteria affect
  representation.
- **Method:** Empirical analysis of the 2001–02 congressional redistricting
  cycle.
- **Key finding:** Districting criteria can pull in different directions, and
  compactness in particular matters for representational outcomes.
- **Relevance:** Motivates why neighborhood integrity may vary across district
  systems instead of being uniformly prioritized.

### Chen and Rodden (2015) — Cutting Through the Thicket
- **Main contribution:** Uses simulation benchmarks to separate partisan
  advantage from legitimate districting objectives.
- **Method:** Algorithmic redistricting benchmark analysis.
- **Key finding:** Analysts need explicit empirical benchmarks when plans trade
  off among competing districting principles, including communities of interest.
- **Relevance:** Supports using neighborhood splitting as a descriptive
  benchmark rather than a self-evident legal conclusion.

## Thematic synthesis

### Neighborhood measurement

The neighborhood-measurement literature argues that local context matters but is
hard to measure consistently. Ansolabehere et al. (2025) solve a major part of
that problem by building a large, city-defined neighborhood dataset. Wong et
al. (2020) reinforces the underlying point: meaningful community geography is
often locally perceived and socially constructed, not simply inherited from
administrative convenience.

### Communities of interest and districting

The redistricting literature treats communities of interest as important but
ambiguous. Cross (1998) shows that even in litigation the concept can point to
different underlying logics. Barabas and Jerit (2004) and Chen and Rodden
(2015) highlight that districting objectives compete with one another, making
descriptive benchmarks especially valuable.

### Gap and opportunity

There is little systematic evidence on whether recognized neighborhood
boundaries are actually preserved in district systems. This paper fills that gap
by comparing city-defined neighborhoods directly to state legislative and city
council boundaries.

## Gaps and next steps

1. Keep the argument descriptive and transparent rather than overclaiming
   normative optimality.
2. Compare district types on the same matched city set whenever council
   coverage is narrower.
3. If feasible, treat population-weighted containment as an extension rather
   than the baseline estimand.
